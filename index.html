Star Clusters and Stellar Evolution · Interactive stellar astronomy for the classroom

About this project

The browser-based tools presented here are for exploring real stellar data from the Gaia mission. No installation required — everything runs in your browser.

The tools are designed to guide students through the process astronomers use to study star clusters: isolating cluster members from foreground and background field stars, reading color–magnitude diagrams, and fitting theoretical stellar models to real data.

Background science

Open star clusters

Open clusters are groups of hundreds to thousands of stars that formed together from the same molecular cloud, and are loosely gravitationally bound. Because they share a common origin, all stars in a cluster have the same age, initial chemical composition, and distance — making them ideal laboratories for testing models of stellar evolution. Famous examples include the Pleiades (∼125 Myr), the Hyades (∼700 Myr), and the older M67 (∼4 Gyr).

Gaia proper motions and parallaxes

The ESA Gaia spacecraft measures the precise positions, motions, and distances of over a billion stars. Stars belonging to the same cluster move together through space, so they form a tight clump in proper-motion space (pmRA vs pmDec) that stands out clearly from the distributed field-star population. Their parallaxes (and therefore distances) are also consistent with one another, giving a second independent way to identify members.

The color–magnitude diagram

When you plot stellar brightness against color (a proxy for surface temperature), stars follow a well-defined main sequence from hot blue stars at the top-left to cool red stars at the bottom-right. As a cluster ages, more massive stars evolve off the main sequence toward the giant branch. The point where stars begin to leave the main sequence — the "turn-off point" — is a sensitive clock: the older the cluster, the lower (cooler, dimmer) the turn-off.

Stellar evolutionary tracks and isochrones

Theoretical models predict how a star of a given mass and composition moves through the HR diagram over its lifetime — these are called evolutionary tracks. An isochrone (from Greek: equal time) connects the positions of stars of different masses but the same age, tracing what a cluster's CMD should look like at that moment. Fitting an isochrone to an observed cluster CMD gives estimates of the cluster's age, distance, and metal abundance.

Workflow

The intended student workflow moves left to right through the three apps:

  1. Use the stellar track explorer to get familiar with isochrones and understand what parameters shift them on the CMD.
  2. Use the cluster finder to select likely cluster members using proper-motion lasso and parallax range filtering.
  3. Use the track overplotter — opened directly from the cluster finder via "Send to overplotter" — to fit an isochrone to your cluster stars and determine its age, distance, and metallicity.

All selections are encoded in the page URL, so sessions can be bookmarked, shared, or submitted as part of a lab report.
